About AAC
What is AAC?⌄
AAC stands for Augmentative and Alternative Communication. It refers to all the ways people communicate besides spoken words — including gestures, facial expressions, sign language, picture symbols, and speech-generating apps on tablets. Many people use a combination of these tools alongside speech. AAC is not one thing — it is a whole toolkit that supports communication in whatever way works best for each person.
Will AAC replace my child's speech?⌄
No. AAC does not replace speech, and research consistently shows it does not slow speech development. In fact, many studies show that access to AAC tools supports the growth of natural speech over time. Your child's team will always encourage and celebrate every form of communication your child uses — including natural speech, vocalizations, gestures, and the device. The goal is to give your child more ways to communicate, not fewer.
My child already uses some words. Why do they need a device?⌄
Many students who use some words also benefit from an AAC device. Speech can be inconsistent — it may work well in some situations and not others. A device gives your child a reliable way to communicate even on days when speech is harder, or in situations where words are difficult to find quickly. Having both available means your child always has a way to be heard. There is no minimum or maximum amount of speech that determines whether AAC is helpful.
What is Proloquo2Go?⌄
Proloquo2Go is the AAC app used in RUSD classrooms. It runs on an iPad and contains thousands of picture-symbol buttons that speak words and phrases out loud when tapped. The app is widely used by students, adults, and people of all ages around the world. It can be customized to match a student's vocabulary, language level, and communication needs. It is one of the most well-researched AAC apps available.
The RUSD AAC Program
What is the RUSD AAC Program?⌄
The RUSD AAC Program provides every Extensive Support Needs (ESN) classroom with iPads loaded with Proloquo2Go. The classroom teacher has a device to model communication throughout the day, and student devices are available for all students to use. Any student who needs to communicate can use a device — no evaluation or IEP goal is required to have access. The program is led by Jerry Butanda, M.S.-SLP, ATAC, AAC, District AAC Lead SLP.
Who can use the classroom device?⌄
Any student in the classroom can use a device. The program is designed so that communication support is available to everyone, not just students with specific IEP goals. No student has to wait, qualify, or ask permission to use a device.
Why does the device stay at school?⌄
Right now the classroom devices are shared tools — similar to books or manipulatives — that all students access throughout the day. Keeping them at school ensures they are always charged, available, and ready. If your child's IEP team determines that a personal take-home device would support their communication at home, that decision is made together with you through the IEP process.
Can my child get their own personal device?⌄
Yes, this is possible. If your child's speech-language pathologist determines — through observation, data, and collaboration with the team — that your child needs a dedicated personal device, a formal AAC evaluation can be completed. If the evaluation supports it, the IEP team can authorize a personal device. This process is guided by your child's individual needs, not a waiting list or budget limit.
What You Can Do at Home
How can I support my child's communication at home?⌄
  • Respond to everything your child communicates — gestures, sounds, words, signs, or device use all count.
  • Give your child time to respond before jumping in. A pause of 10–15 seconds can make a big difference.
  • Ask your child's SLP to show you the app so you can use it together at home during meals, play, or routines.
  • Talk to your child's teacher about what vocabulary is being practiced at school so you can reinforce it at home.
  • Most importantly — celebrate every communication attempt, no matter how it happens.
My child uses signs at home. Should I tell the school?⌄
Yes, absolutely. Signs, gestures, and any other communication forms your child uses at home are valuable and important. Please share this information with your child's SLP so that the school can recognize and respond to those forms too. Your child's total communication system — everything they use to communicate — should be supported consistently between home and school.
What if my child refuses to use the device?⌄
This is very common, especially at first. AAC takes time — just like learning any new skill. The classroom team is trained to offer the device without pressure and to respond to all communication attempts regardless of whether the device is used. If you have concerns about engagement, talk to your child's SLP. There are many strategies for building comfort and motivation with a device, and the team will work with you on this.
Working With the Team
Who is my child's speech-language pathologist?⌄
Each school site has a speech-language pathologist who supports students with communication needs. Your child's SLP coordinates AAC support at the classroom level and communicates with the District AAC Lead SLP. If you are not sure who your child's SLP is, contact your school's front office or reach out to Jerry Butanda at jbutanda@rialtousd.org.
Can I observe my child using the device in the classroom?⌄
Yes. You are welcome to request a classroom observation. Contact your child's teacher or SLP to schedule a time. Seeing the program in action is one of the best ways to understand how your child communicates at school and how you can support that at home.
